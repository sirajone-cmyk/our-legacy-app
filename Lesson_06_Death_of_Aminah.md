# Lesson 6 — The Death of Āminah
## The Prophet ﷺ Loses His Mother

**Series:** OUR LEGACY Sīrah Series
**Lesson Number:** 6
**Theme:** Allāh shelters those He has chosen — even through loss

---

بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ

---

## Opening

He had returned from the desert.

The years with Ḥalīmah al-Saʿdiyyah and the tribe of Banū Saʿd had come to an end. The young Muḥammad ﷺ — barely five or six years old — was back in Makkah, back in the arms of his mother, Āminah bint Wahb.

For a short time, there was peace.

But Allāh ﷻ had decreed a journey for this child — not only across the Arabian desert, but through loss, grief, and hardship — so that when the weight of prophethood finally came, he would already know what it meant to face the world with nothing but trust in Allāh.

This is the story of Āminah bint Wahb — a noble, beloved, devoted mother — and of the day her young son stood at her side as she left this world.

It is a lesson about loss. About patience. About the mercy of Allāh ﷻ who shelters those He loves even when everything around them falls away.

---

## Virtues Section

### The Qurʾān Acknowledges the Orphan

Allāh ﷻ Himself speaks of this moment — this loss — in Sūrah al-Ḍuḥā:

**Arabic:**

أَلَمْ يَجِدْكَ يَتِيمًا فَآوَىٰ

**Transliteration:**
*Alam yajidka yatīman fa-āwā*

**Translation:**
"Did He not find you an orphan and give you shelter?"
*(Sūrah al-Ḍuḥā, 93:6)*

**Reference:** Qurʾān — Sūrah al-Ḍuḥā (93), Verse 6

**Short Explanation:**
This verse was revealed in Makkah after the Prophet ﷺ had already received prophethood. Allāh ﷻ reminds His Prophet of His care and protection through every stage of his life — including the years of orphanhood. The word *yatīm* in Arabic refers to a child who has lost a parent. When his father ʿAbdullāh died before his birth, he became a *yatīm* in one sense. When his mother Āminah died, that orphanhood was complete. Yet Allāh says: *I sheltered you.*

**Practical Lesson:**
When a child loses a parent, or when any person feels completely alone, this verse is a reminder that Allāh sees, and Allāh provides shelter. No orphan is truly without a guardian when Allāh is their guardian.

**Arabic:**

**Translation:**
"And We will surely test you with something of fear and hunger and a loss of wealth and lives and fruits, but give good tidings to the patient — who, when disaster strikes them, say: 'Indeed we belong to Allāh, and indeed to Him we will return.'"
*(Sūrah al-Baqarah, 2:155–156)*

**Note:** This verse was revealed later in Madīnah, but it describes the principle that Allāh ﷻ tests those He loves — and the response of the believer is patience and return to Him.

## Main Story

### Part One — Who Was Āminah?

Before we speak of her death, we must know her life.

Āminah bint Wahb came from one of the most respected families in Makkah. Her father, Wahb ibn ʿAbd Manāf ibn Zuhra, was a chief of his clan — the tribe of Zuhra. Her family was known for nobility, honour, and good character.

When she married ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — the most beloved son of the great chieftain of Makkah — it was considered one of the finest marriages of that generation.

But her joy was to be short-lived.

ʿAbdullāh set out on a trading journey to Shām (Syria). On the return, he fell ill. He stopped in Yathrib — the city that would one day be called Madīnah — where his maternal relatives, the clan of Banū Najjār, lived. There, far from his family, far from Makkah, far from the wife who was carrying his child — ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib passed away.

Āminah received the news while still pregnant.

She gave birth alone, widowed, grieving — and yet the books of Sīrah tell us she carried herself with dignity and patience. She named her son Muḥammad.

She raised him. She loved him. She told him about his father.

And when the time came for him to spend his early years with the wet-nurse Ḥalīmah al-Saʿdiyyah among the tribe of Banū Saʿd — as was the noble custom of the Arabs of Makkah — she agreed, though it meant separation.

When he returned, she held him close.

She was now a widow, a single mother in a society where such things were not easy, and yet she was described in the books of Sīrah as a woman of nobility, dignity, and deep affection for her son.

---

### Part Two — The Journey to Yathrib

Some time after the young Muḥammad ﷺ returned from Banū Saʿd — the books of Sīrah tell us he was approximately six years old — his mother Āminah made a decision.

She would travel to Yathrib.

There were two reasons: to visit the grave of his father ʿAbdullāh, so that her son could know where his father was buried. And to visit her maternal relatives — some reports mention the clan of Banū ʿAdī ibn al-Najjār — who lived in that city.

It was a long journey. From Makkah to Yathrib is approximately five hundred kilometres through the desert. But Āminah was determined.

She prepared for the journey. Accompanying her were:

- Her young son, Muḥammad ﷺ
- **Umm Ayman** — also known as Barakah al-Ḥabashiyyah — the devoted Ethiopian servant who had cared for the household since the time of ʿAbdullāh. The Prophet ﷺ would later call her *"my mother after my mother."*
- ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — his grandfather — is mentioned by some sources as arranging or being aware of the journey.

They set out on camelback, following the ancient caravan road north through the Ḥijāz desert.

*The books of Sīrah record this journey, though detailed narrations of specific moments along the way are not extensively preserved.*

---

### Part Three — Yathrib: A Month Among Family

When they arrived in Yathrib, they were welcomed.

The young Muḥammad ﷺ — for the first time in his life — visited the city that Allāh would one day make the greatest city on earth. He played with the children of Banū Najjār. He walked the streets. He breathed air that would one day be the air of the City of the Prophet ﷺ.

The books of Sīrah mention that he learnt to swim in a pool belonging to the clan of Banū Najjār during this visit. He spent time with his maternal relatives and, perhaps most importantly, he stood at the grave of his father — the grave of ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib.

What did the young child feel as he stood at that grave? We are not told. But Allāh knows.

They remained in Yathrib for approximately one month. Then Āminah prepared for the return journey home to Makkah.

---

### Part Four — Illness on the Road

The return journey began.

But somewhere on the road heading south, Āminah bint Wahb fell ill.

The desert heat, the long journey, the years of grief she had carried since ʿAbdullāh's death — whatever Allāh had decreed, her body began to weaken.

They reached a place called **Al-Abwāʾ** — a village lying between Makkah and Madīnah, closer to the Madīnah side. It was here that the caravan stopped.

It was here that Āminah could travel no further.

---

### Part Five — The Death of Āminah

At Al-Abwāʾ, Āminah bint Wahb passed from this world.

The books of Sīrah record that she died during this return journey. She was young — scholars estimate she was in her mid-to-late twenties. Her son was six years old.

Muḥammad ﷺ was present.

She was buried there, at Al-Abwāʾ, in the earth of the Ḥijāz — far from Makkah, far from the grave of her husband — in a place that would become known to all because of the love her son ﷺ carried for her throughout his life.

Umm Ayman, with her own eyes weeping, took the young orphan's hand.

---

### Part Six — The Return to Makkah

Umm Ayman brought the young Muḥammad ﷺ back to Makkah.

He arrived at the home of his grandfather, ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — the great chieftain of Quraysh, the old man who had wept with joy at his birth, who had named him Muḥammad, who had watched over him since his father's death.

Now his mother was gone too.

The books of Sīrah tell us that ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib received his grandson with sorrow and love, and took him under his full care and protection.

The young Muḥammad ﷺ — six years old, an orphan without father or mother — entered the house of his grandfather.

And Allāh ﷻ was watching over him.

---

### Part Seven — The Visit to the Grave (After Prophethood)

Decades later, after prophethood had come and the message of Islām was spreading across Arabia — the Prophet ﷺ passed by Al-Abwāʾ.

He stopped.

He visited his mother's grave.

What happened there is recorded authentically in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim:

> *"The Prophet ﷺ visited his mother's grave. He wept, and caused those around him to weep. He then said: 'I asked my Lord's permission to seek forgiveness for her, but I was not given permission. And I asked His permission to visit her grave, and I was given permission. So visit graves, for they remind you of death.'"*
> *(Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, Ḥadīth 976)*

The Prophet ﷺ wept at his mother's grave.

This is one of the most moving moments in the entire Sīrah. The man who bore the message of the entire universe — who spoke to kings, who led armies, who received revelation from Allāh — stood at a grave in the desert and wept for his mother.

He was not ashamed of his tears.

He was not told that weeping is weakness.

His tears were love. His tears were human. His tears were the mark of a heart that remembered.

## Reflection Questions

1. Āminah was a young widow who raised the Prophet ﷺ alone and then passed away on a journey. What does her life teach us about patience and sacrifice?

2. The Prophet ﷺ wept at his mother's grave even after prophethood. What does this tell us about how Islām views grief and emotion?

3. Allāh allowed the Prophet ﷺ to experience losing both parents before the age of seven. Why do you think Allāh allowed this? What did it prepare him for?

4. Umm Ayman walked the young orphan back to Makkah alone. What qualities must she have had? What do we learn about those who care for children in need?

5. The Prophet ﷺ said: "Visit graves, for they remind you of death." How can thinking about death make us *better* people while we are alive?

6. If you were six years old and lost your mother in a faraway place, how would you feel? And how does it feel to know that the greatest human being who ever lived went through exactly that?

## Family Discussion

- Our Prophet ﷺ grew up without his father, then without his mother. Yet he became the most complete human being who ever lived. What does this tell us about where our strength comes from?

- Is there someone in our community — a widow, an orphan, someone who has lost a loved one — who needs our care and attention? What can our family do?

- The Prophet ﷺ wept for his mother at her grave. When is the last time we made duʿāʾ for our parents? Let us do that together right now.

- What qualities did Āminah bint Wahb need to raise the Prophet ﷺ during those early years? Which of those qualities should our family build?

## Authentication Notes

### Established Facts
*(Authenticated in the books of Ḥadīth or well-attested across multiple reliable Sīrah sources)*

✓ The Prophet ﷺ was born after his father ʿAbdullāh's death — his father died in Yathrib before his birth.
✓ The Prophet ﷺ lost his mother Āminah at approximately 6 years of age — this is accepted across all primary sources.
✓ Āminah died at a place called Al-Abwāʾ on the road between Makkah and Madīnah.
✓ The Prophet ﷺ visited his mother's grave after prophethood, wept, and taught about visiting graves — authenticated in Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim (Ḥadīth 976).
✓ Umm Ayman (Barakah) was a devoted servant of the household and cared for the Prophet ﷺ in his early life — established across multiple sources.
✓ The Prophet ﷺ expressed deep love and respect for Umm Ayman throughout his life — he called her "my mother after my mother."
✓ The Prophet ﷺ went to live with his grandfather ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib after his mother's death.

### Widely Accepted Reports
*(Preserved in the books of Sīrah with broad acceptance among scholars, though not in the main ḥadīth collections)*

◎ Āminah made the journey to Yathrib to visit the grave of ʿAbdullāh and her relatives in the clan of Banū Najjār.
◎ The Prophet ﷺ was approximately 6 years old at the time of his mother's death.
◎ The journey lasted approximately one month in Yathrib before the return trip began.
◎ The young Prophet ﷺ played with children of Banū Najjār and learned to swim during this visit — mentioned in some Sīrah accounts.

### Reports Discussed by Scholars
*(Found in some sources; treated with care)*

⚠ Some accounts describe specific conversations between Āminah and her son during the journey — these are not preserved with strong chains and should not be presented as established fact.

⚠ The specific circumstances of Āminah's illness and the exact duration of her dying are not precisely narrated. We know she died at Al-Abwāʾ; the details surrounding that death are not well-preserved.

⚠ The theological status of Āminah has been discussed by scholars. This is a matter of scholarly discussion. The Prophet ﷺ was not permitted to seek forgiveness for her after prophethood (Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim 976), which scholars discuss in the context of those who died before the message reached them. This is a complex scholarly topic, not appropriate for general family taʿlīm without proper scholarly guidance. In this lesson, we focus on the love the Prophet ﷺ showed for his mother and the human experience of grief — without entering into theological debate about her final status.

## Explore Further

### Why did this happen?

Allāh ﷻ chose to prepare His Prophet ﷺ through a childhood of loss. He lost his father before he was born. He was raised by a wet-nurse in the desert. He experienced the Shaqq al-Ṣadr. He returned to his mother — only for her to leave the world when he was six years old.

Each loss was a preparation. The Prophet ﷺ would grow up to be the greatest advocate for orphans, widows, the poor, and the bereaved in human history. He knew their pain — not as theory, but as lived experience. His mercy was real because his loss was real.

### What happened next?

After the death of Āminah, the Prophet ﷺ went to live with his grandfather ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — the respected chieftain of the Quraysh. Under his grandfather's care, he was loved, honoured, and protected. But ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib was already an elderly man. Two years later, when the Prophet ﷺ was approximately eight years old, ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib too would pass away — and the young Muḥammad ﷺ would enter the care of his uncle, Abū Ṭālib.

**[Next Lesson: Under the Wing of ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — and His Death]**

### Why was this important?

The death of Āminah is not merely a biographical detail. It is the moment the Prophet ﷺ became a full orphan — a *yatīm* without both father and mother. Allāh ﷻ would later command the believers no fewer than 23 times in the Qurʾān to honour, protect, and care for orphans. The man who delivered that command had lived that reality.

The Prophet ﷺ weeping at his mother's grave — decades after her death — shows us that grief does not expire. Love does not expire. And the permission Allāh gave him to visit her grave shows us that Allāh honours the love between a child and a parent.

### Which personalities should we study next?

- **ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib ibn Hāshim** — the grandfather who raised the young Prophet ﷺ, and the next great figure in his early life
- **Umm Ayman (Barakah al-Ḥabashiyyah)** — the loyal servant who stayed with the Prophet ﷺ his entire life, married, had children, and whom he called *"my mother after my mother"*
- **Abū Ṭālib ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib** — the uncle who would receive the Prophet ﷺ after ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib's death and protect him through the most difficult years of the Makkan period
