# OUR LEGACY — SĪRAH SERIES
## Lesson: The Year of the Elephant and the Birth of Rasūlullāh ﷺ

---

## 1. TITLE

**THE YEAR OF THE ELEPHANT**
**AND THE BIRTH OF THE FINAL MESSENGER ﷺ**

*The world was about to change forever.*

---

## 2. OPENING

We left our last lesson at a remarkable moment.

After tracing the lineage of the Prophet ﷺ back through Hāshim, Quraysh, Ismāʿīl, and Ibrāhīm — through centuries of preserved family lines and sacred ground — we finally arrived at two people. A young man named ʿAbdullāh and a young woman named Āminah. The father and mother of the most important human being who would ever walk the earth.

You might expect us to move immediately to the birth. But history rarely moves that way. Before the greatest event in human history, Allāh ﷻ arranged a dramatic sign — one so remarkable that the Arabs named an entire year after it. That year became the reference point by which they remembered everything else.

They called it *ʿĀm al-Fīl* — the Year of the Elephant.

And it was in that very year that Rasūlullāh ﷺ was born.

---

## 3. MAIN STORY

### The World That Was Waiting

By the time ʿAbdullāh and Āminah were married, Makkah was already one of the most important cities in Arabia. Its importance did not come from its size or its wealth alone — it came from something far older than either of those things. At the centre of Makkah stood the Kaʿbah — the House of Allāh. The structure that Ibrāhīm ʿalayhi us-salām had built by divine command, in the same valley where he had left his family, in the same place where Zamzam had gushed from the earth.

The Arabs came to Makkah from every corner of the peninsula. They came to visit the House, to trade, to settle disputes. Whatever their beliefs — and many of them had drifted far from the pure faith of Ibrāhīm — they still held the Kaʿbah with a respect that went beyond ordinary things. It was as though something in the human soul recognised the sanctity of the place, even when the mind had forgotten why.

Quraysh — the tribe of ʿAbdullāh and Āminah, the tribe of ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — were the custodians of this House. This was their honour, their responsibility, and their identity.

And this is the setting into which Rasūlullāh ﷺ was about to be born.

But first, someone tried to destroy it all.

---

### The Man Who Wanted to Tear Down the Kaʿbah

Far to the south, across the sea, the land of Yemen was under the rule of a powerful governor named Abraha. He was an Abyssinian — a man of great ambition and enormous military strength. He had built, in the city of Sanʿāʾ, a magnificent church — a structure of breathtaking beauty, decorated with gold and precious materials, designed to be the most impressive religious building the world had ever seen.

His purpose was political as much as it was religious. He wanted the Arabs to stop travelling north to Makkah and to come south to his church instead. He wanted to redirect the flow of pilgrims — and with them, the flow of prestige and wealth — away from the Kaʿbah and toward his own monument.

It did not work. The Arabs were not interested.

This did not please Abraha.

When a man from one of the Arab tribes desecrated Abraha's church in protest, Abraha's frustration turned into fury. He made a decision that he believed would solve the problem permanently: he would march to Makkah and destroy the Kaʿbah itself.

He assembled a vast army. At the front of his forces, he placed war elephants — great, towering creatures that no ordinary army of the time could stand against. The sight of an elephant in battle was enough to terrify men and scatter horses. Abraha's lead elephant was called Maḥmūd.

The army moved north toward Makkah. Tribe after tribe along the route either stepped aside or was crushed. No one could stop them. By the time word reached Makkah, the city was in a state of fear.

---

### The Man Who Stood Between the Army and the House

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — the grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ, the most respected man in Makkah — was the one who went out to meet Abraha.

Before the army had arrived at the outskirts of Makkah, Abraha's troops had swept through the surrounding area and taken camels. Among the camels taken were two hundred that belonged to ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib himself.

Word was sent to ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib: Abraha would see him.

When ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib was brought before the commander, Abraha looked at him with a mixture of respect and surprise. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib was a man of remarkable dignity — tall, eloquent, and deeply composed. Abraha had him seated beside him as an equal.

Abraha expected ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib to plead for the Kaʿbah. He expected the custodian of the House to beg, to negotiate, to argue. He had prepared his response for that conversation.

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib said nothing of the sort. He spoke only about his camels.

He asked Abraha to return the two hundred camels that his soldiers had taken.

Abraha was stunned. He said — and his words carry a deep significance — *"I looked at you and I respected you. But now you have spoken, and I think less of you. You come to speak to me about camels, while I have come to destroy the house that is the religion of your fathers?"*

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib replied with words that have echoed through history.

*"I am the lord of the camels. The House has a Lord who will protect it."*

That was the entire argument. He would not plead for the Kaʿbah. It was not his place. The House belonged to Allāh ﷻ. Let Allāh ﷻ defend His own House. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib's job was to ask for what was his.

Abraha returned the camels.

---

### The Evacuation of Makkah

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib returned to Makkah. He went to the Kaʿbah. He took hold of the ring of the door — and he made duʿāʾ. He called upon Allāh ﷻ to protect His House, to defend what no human army could defend.

Then he gave the order: the people of Makkah were to leave. They would go to the mountains and the high ground surrounding the city and watch from there. No one was to stay and fight. This was not a battle that human hands could win.

Makkah was emptied.

The people waited in the hills.

Abraha's army began its final advance toward the city.

---

### The Morning of the Birds

The lead elephant, Maḥmūd, walked to the edge of Makkah.

And then it stopped.

The elephant handlers beat it, prodded it, tried to force it forward. Maḥmūd would not move. He would sit, sinking his weight down, refusing to advance. When they turned him to face south, north, east — he would walk. When they turned him to face the Kaʿbah, he sat down and would not move.

Then something appeared on the horizon.

The sky filled with birds — flocks of them, coming in waves from the direction of the sea. Each bird carried three stones: one in its beak and one in each talon. The stones were small. They looked like dried clay — hardened pellets, no bigger than a chickpea.

The birds flew over the army of Abraha and released the stones.

Where the stones fell, they did not simply bruise. The men they struck were destroyed. The army that had marched from Yemen — the most powerful military force the Arabian peninsula had seen — was left in ruin. Abraha himself was struck and retreated, dying before he could return home. His army was like a field of crops that had been eaten away, leaving only hollow stalks.

This event is not preserved only in historical accounts. Allāh ﷻ revealed a chapter of the Qurʾān about it — five short verses that every Muslim knows, that children memorise, that families recite. He asked: *"Have you not seen what your Lord did to the people of the Elephant?"*

That question is not rhetorical. It is an invitation to reflection that has never expired. We are still meant to look at that event and understand what it tells us about the nature of power and the nature of Allāh's protection.

The Kaʿbah stood.

The elephants were gone.

The birds returned to wherever they had come from.

---

### Why Allāh Chose This Moment

Here is something to sit with.

The destruction of Abraha's army and the birth of Rasūlullāh ﷺ happened in the same year. Not in the same century. Not in the same decade. In the same year. Some accounts connect them even more closely — within months of each other.

This is not coincidence.

When we think about it, the connection becomes clear. The Kaʿbah was the house that Ibrāhīm ʿalayhi us-salām had built by divine command, in the city where Ismāʿīl had grown, from whose lineage Rasūlullāh ﷺ would come. That House was about to become, once more, the focal point of the world's faith — after centuries of being surrounded by idols and neglect. The Messenger who would restore it to its original purpose was about to be born.

Allāh ﷻ did not allow that House to be destroyed in the year His final Messenger came into the world. He protected it visibly, miraculously, unmistakably — so that the event would be remembered, so that the year would be marked, so that the Arabs would have no excuse to say that the birth of the Prophet ﷺ had passed unnoticed by history.

The birth of Rasūlullāh ﷺ arrived wrapped in a year that no one would ever forget.

---

### The Loss That Came Before

But before we reach that birth, there is a grief to acknowledge.

ʿAbdullāh — the young father, the son of ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — did not live to see his child.

He had gone on a journey to Shām, travelling for trade as the men of Quraysh did. On his return, he fell ill in the city of Yathrib — the city that would one day be called Madīnah. He stayed there, in the home of his maternal relatives, hoping to recover. He did not recover.

ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib died in Yathrib. He was a young man — some say around twenty-five years old. He left behind him a wife who was pregnant, a father who was grieving, and a legacy he could not have imagined: the child that Āminah was carrying.

When word reached Makkah, the family mourned. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — who had already sacrificed so much, who had already given so many years to the service of the Kaʿbah — now lost his beloved son. And Āminah was left to carry the remainder of her pregnancy without her husband.

She was alone. She was grieving. And within her, unbeknownst to her in its full significance, she was carrying the greatest trust Allāh ﷻ had ever placed in a human womb.

---

### The Birth

In the month of Rabīʿ al-Awwal, in the Year of the Elephant, in the city of Makkah — a child was born.

The day was a Monday. This is something Rasūlullāh ﷺ confirmed himself, when he was asked about the significance of fasting on Mondays. He said that Monday was the day he was born — and also the day the revelation first came to him. Monday held a meaning for him that went beyond ordinary time.

The child was born to Āminah bint Wahb, in the house of his family in Makkah. His grandfather ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib was sent for. The old man who had stood before Abraha, who had held the ring of the Kaʿbah door and called upon Allāh to protect His House — he now held in his arms the child who would one day call all of humanity back to Allāh.

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib carried the infant to the Kaʿbah. He stood in the House and gave thanks. He made duʿāʾ. And then he gave the child a name that no Arab child had been given before: Muḥammad.

When people asked him why he had chosen a name not common among them, he said that he wanted this child to be praised — by the people of the earth and by Allāh in the heavens. The name Muḥammad means *the one who is praised* or *the one who is repeatedly praised*. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib did not know, in the full sense, what he was naming. But Allāh ﷻ guided that name.

The child who arrived without a father, to a grieving mother, in a city that had just witnessed a miracle — would grow up to become the human being about whom Allāh ﷻ said:

*"And We have sent you only as a mercy to all the worlds."*

That is who was born on that Monday in Makkah.

That is Muḥammad ﷺ.

## 5. WHAT WE LEARN

**Allāh protects what He has chosen.**
The Kaʿbah was not protected because the people of Makkah were powerful. It was not protected because Quraysh had a great army. It was protected because Allāh willed to protect it. When Allāh decides to safeguard something, no force on earth can overcome that decision.

**Dignity in difficulty is a mark of true faith.**
ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib did not panic. He did not beg. He stood before the most powerful military commander in the region and spoke with quiet certainty: *"The House has a Lord who will protect it."* He did not know how. He did not know when. But he knew *who*. That kind of trust — calm, unshakeable, rooted in the knowledge of Allāh — is the inheritance of people who truly know their Lord.

**Grief and greatness can arrive together.**
Āminah lost her husband while carrying the most significant child in human history. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib lost his son in the same year he would become the grandfather of the final Prophet. Life's greatest blessings are often wrapped in its deepest sorrows. This does not mean Allāh has abandoned us. It means He trusts us with weight.

**Allāh names His servants with purpose.**
The name Muḥammad — chosen by a grandfather standing at the door of the Kaʿbah — was not an accident. Every name in this story means something. And the name of the one whose praise fills the heavens and the earth is: *the one who is praised*.

## 6. REFLECTION QUESTIONS

1. ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib told Abraha: *"The House has a Lord who will protect it."* What does this tell us about where he placed his trust — and what can we learn from that for our own lives?

2. Allāh chose the Year of the Elephant — a year of crisis and miraculous deliverance — as the year to send His final Prophet. What might that timing be telling us?

3. Āminah was pregnant, alone, and grieving — and yet she was carrying the greatest blessing in human history. How does this change the way we think about difficult times in our own lives?

4. The Prophet ﷺ told us he was born on a Monday. He also received the first revelation on a Monday. What does it mean to mark time with significance — and how do we create that in our families?

---

## 7. FAMILY DISCUSSION

**Gather together and discuss:**

ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib returned the people's camels, told the people to leave the city, held the door of the Kaʿbah, and made duʿāʾ. Then he waited.

He did everything he could. And then he left the rest to Allāh.

**Discuss with your family:**
- Is there a situation in our lives right now where we have done everything we can — and the rest is in Allāh's hands?
- What would it look like to have the kind of certainty ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib had — not panicking, not despairing, but simply trusting?
- How do we teach our children the difference between giving up and handing something to Allāh?

## 8. ACTION POINT

**This week:** Read Sūrah al-Fīl together as a family — all five verses. Read the meaning in English. Then talk about it for five minutes: *What happened? What did Allāh want us to remember? Why is this chapter still in the Qurʾān for us, today, in our time?*

Make it a habit: every time one of your children memorises a new sūrah, read the meaning together. The Qurʾān was not only meant to be recited — it was meant to be understood.
