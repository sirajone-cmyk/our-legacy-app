# OUR LEGACY — SĪRAH SERIES
## Lesson 4: Ḥalīmah al-Saʿdiyyah and the Desert Years

**Series:** The Seal of the Prophets ﷺ — The Greatest Life Ever Lived
**Lesson:** 4 of the Sīrah Journey
**Reading Time:** Approximately 12–15 minutes
**Suitable for:** Family Taʿlīm · Classroom · Read-Aloud Narration

---

## OPENING — CONNECTING TO THE PREVIOUS LESSON

In our last lesson, we stood in the city of Makkah in a year the Arabs would never forget.

We watched as an army of war elephants approached the Kaʿbah — and was destroyed by birds carrying stones. We watched as an old man stood before a mighty commander and said, without flinching: *"The House has a Lord who will protect it."* And then, in the quiet that followed that extraordinary year, we witnessed the arrival of a child.

He was born on a Monday. His grandfather carried him to the Kaʿbah, gave thanks, and gave him a name no Arab child had been given before: Muḥammad — the praised one, the repeatedly praised one.

His father had passed away before he was born. He arrived to a mother who was grieving. He arrived into a world that did not yet know what it had received.

And within days of his birth, the first of a series of extraordinary arrangements began — arrangements that Allāh ﷻ had put in place long before anyone knew his name.

---

## MAIN STORY

### Section One: A Custom That Carried Wisdom

To understand what comes next, we need to understand a practice that was common among the noble families of Makkah.

The city was a trading hub — busy, noisy, and crowded. The air of the cities was considered heavy, the dialects mixed, and the environment of urban life was seen as weakening for young children. The great families of Arabia had a long-standing tradition: when a child was born, they would arrange for him or her to be sent to live with a Bedouin family in the open desert for the first few years of life.

This was not abandonment. It was care — deeply considered care.

The desert offered things the city could not. Clean air and open land. Physical strength, built through a harder life. And above all, the Arabic language in its purest form. The Bedouin tribes spoke an Arabic untouched by the mixing of traders from Persia, Yemen, and Shām. A child raised among them would grow up speaking with clarity, precision, and beauty. For a family that valued eloquence — and Quraysh prized eloquence above almost everything — this was a priceless gift.

Every year, women from the surrounding desert tribes would make the journey to Makkah. They came as wet-nurses, offering to take infants back to their tribes in exchange for payment from the families. It was an arrangement that worked on both sides: the families got what they wanted for their children, and the women earned an income in difficult years.

The year Rasūlullāh ﷺ was born was a particularly difficult year. A drought had settled over the desert.

### Educational Note: Why Did Allāh Choose Banū Saʿd?

The sending of Rasūlullāh ﷺ to the tribe of Banū Saʿd was not simply a social custom. Looking back, we can see at least five reasons why this was part of a divine arrangement:

**Pure Arabic at its source.** The dialect of Banū Saʿd was considered among the clearest and most eloquent in all of Arabia. A child raised among them would grow up speaking with precision and beauty — a gift that would serve Rasūlullāh ﷺ when he one day stood before all of humanity as its final Messenger.

**Physical strength through a harder life.** The desert built resilience. Rasūlullāh ﷺ would face decades of difficulty ahead — persecution, migration, battles, and grief. The desert years built the body and the character that would carry that weight.

**Distance from the distractions of the city.** Makkah was full of idols, noise, and the moral confusion of a busy trading city. The desert offered simplicity, space, and an upbringing free from those influences.

**Clean air and open land.** Away from the crowding and the diseases of city life, the child grew healthy and strong.

**Divine preparation.** Allāh ﷻ does not send His Prophets unprepared. Every arrangement in the early life of Rasūlullāh ﷺ — including the desert years — was part of a preparation we are only now able to look back and understand.

### Section Two: The Women of Banū Saʿd Arrive in Makkah

Among the tribes that made this annual journey was the tribe of Banū Saʿd ibn Bakr.

That year, a group of women from Banū Saʿd set out for Makkah. The journey itself told the story of the drought. One of those women, Ḥalīmah bint Abī Dhuʾayb, rode an old she-donkey — slow, tired, barely able to keep pace. Her own infant son cried through the nights of the journey because she herself had little milk to give him. Her husband, al-Ḥārith, accompanied her. They arrived in Makkah thin, weary, and hoping.

The women of Banū Saʿd went from household to household. Makkah's families came out to meet them. Arrangements were made. One by one, the women of Banū Saʿd each found a child to take.

But there was one infant that no one seemed to want.

He was the child of ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib — a young father who had died before his son was born. This child had no living father. And this was a problem, because the custom was clear: the father paid the wet-nurse. No father meant no clear income. The other families had living fathers, living incomes, living futures. This infant had only his mother, his grandfather, and the care of Allāh ﷻ.

One by one, the women looked at this child and moved on.

---

### Section Three: The Woman Who Turned Back

Ḥalīmah had also passed over this child.

When every other woman had found an infant and Ḥalīmah alone had nothing to show for the journey, she turned to her husband. She could not bear the thought of returning to Banū Saʿd empty-handed — the only woman in the group without a child to nurse. That was not just an economic failure. Among the Bedouin, it carried a sense of shame. Her husband said to her: go back. Take the orphan. Perhaps Allāh will bless us through him.

And so Ḥalīmah turned back.

She went to the household of Āminah. She looked at the infant. She picked him up.

And in that moment, before she had even carried him out of the house, something happened that she would spend the rest of her life describing.

She found, when she sat down to nurse him, that her breasts were suddenly full — full in a way they had not been throughout the entire difficult journey. She nursed the infant Muḥammad ﷺ until he was satisfied. She then turned to nurse her own son — and he too was satisfied. Both children slept.

Her husband went to their she-camel to milk her. He found the udders full, where they had been dry before. They drank until they were full that night — something they had not done in a long time.

Her husband looked at her and said: "Ḥalīmah, do you realise that you have taken a blessed child?"

The blessings were becoming impossible to ignore.

---

### Section Four: The Journey Home — and the Blessings That Followed

The morning came, and the group of women from Banū Saʿd prepared to leave Makkah and begin the journey home.

Ḥalīmah's old she-donkey — the one that had lagged behind the entire journey to Makkah, that had exhausted and frustrated the group with its slow, stumbling pace — suddenly walked with such swiftness that the other women could no longer keep up with her. They called out to Ḥalīmah: is that the same donkey you came with? She said: yes, by Allāh — it is the same donkey.

Nothing had changed about the animal. Everything had changed about what she was carrying.

They reached the land of Banū Saʿd.

The barakah did not stop at the journey. It settled into Ḥalīmah's home like a permanent guest. Her sheep went out to graze each day and returned full of milk — while the sheep of her neighbours in Banū Saʿd came back dry. The other members of the tribe began to notice. They started sending their shepherds to graze wherever the shepherds of Ḥalīmah's family went. If her flock grazed on a piece of land, that land gave them something. If her family's animals ate from a pasture, the pasture was generous.

The people of Banū Saʿd witnessed this for two years.

And in those two years, the child grew.

He was not like the other children around him. Ḥalīmah noticed that he grew well, remained healthy, and displayed qualities that often amazed those around him. Ḥalīmah watched him with a mixture of love and wonder that she could not fully put into words.

The two years of nursing passed, and the time came for Ḥalīmah to return Muḥammad ﷺ to his mother in Makkah.

She did not want to go.

When she arrived in Makkah and brought the child to Āminah, Ḥalīmah asked — with sincerity, and with a mother's reluctance — if she could keep the child a little longer. She spoke of the goodness he had brought into her home. She spoke of her worry about the diseases that gathered in city life. Āminah — moved by this love, and reassured by what she saw — agreed. The child was returned to Ḥalīmah. He would remain with the tribe of Banū Saʿd for a further period.

The blessings continued.

## WHAT WE LEARN

**Allāh ﷻ provides through unexpected sources.**
Ḥalīmah set out with nothing and came back with everything — because she took the one child no one else wanted. The blessing was in the one everyone had passed over.

**Barakah is real, and it moves with the righteous.**
The neighbours of Ḥalīmah did not receive what she received, even though they lived on the same land. Barakah is not an equal distribution. It flows toward those whom Allāh ﷻ has willed — and in this case, it flowed because of who was living in that tent.

**Humility before Allāh ﷻ opens doors that pride closes.**
Ḥalīmah's husband said: *go back and take the orphan — perhaps Allāh will bless us through him.* That word "perhaps" is the word of a person who does not claim certainty about Allāh's gifts. He hoped. He trusted. And the hope was answered.

**The Prophet ﷺ was prepared for his mission from his earliest days.**
He did not spend his childhood in the noise and complexity of Makkah. He was raised in open land, clean air, and the purest Arabic. Everything about his early life — even the things that seemed like hardship — was, in hindsight, a preparation.

**An act of kindness toward one person can change a household.**
Āminah entrusted her son to Ḥalīmah. Ḥalīmah opened her home to an orphan. What entered through that door of generosity changed everything in the home — from the milk in the animals to the pace of the donkey on the road.

## REFLECTION QUESTIONS

1. Ḥalīmah almost left Makkah without him. She changed her mind only because she did not want to go home empty-handed. But Allāh ﷻ turned that small, ordinary decision into one of the most blessed moments in the life of Banū Saʿd. Have you ever made a decision for a simple reason — and later realised that Allāh ﷻ had placed barakah inside that choice?

2. The other women of Banū Saʿd chose children from families that had fathers and incomes. They chose what seemed like the sensible option. Ḥalīmah — almost by accident — chose differently. What does this teach us about the difference between what looks promising and what Allāh ﷻ has actually placed blessing inside?

3. The sheep of Ḥalīmah's neighbours came home dry while hers came home full of milk. The neighbours eventually started sending their flocks to graze where Ḥalīmah's flock grazed. They were trying to borrow the barakah without knowing where it came from. What does this tell us about the invisible effects that a blessed person has on the people around them?

4. Ḥalīmah loved this child so much that when the two years ended and the time came to return him to his mother, she asked to keep him longer. What kind of woman must she have been? And what responsibility do we carry when Allāh ﷻ places a trust in our hands?

## FAMILY DISCUSSION

*To be discussed together after reading:*

**For the children:** Ḥalīmah was kind to a child who had no father. She fed him, cared for him, and loved him. Because of her kindness, her whole family was blessed. Can you think of one kind thing you could do this week for someone who needs it?

**For the teenagers:** The other women of Banū Saʿd made what seemed like the smart choice — they took children from wealthier families with fathers who could pay well. Ḥalīmah took the orphan. The "smart choice" gave them an ordinary outcome. The unexpected choice gave her something extraordinary. What does this tell you about how you make decisions?

**For the parents:** Ḥalīmah's husband said: *go back — perhaps Allāh will bless us through him.* He did not know. He hoped. He encouraged. Think of a time when you or your spouse encouraged each other to try something that seemed uncertain — and what Allāh ﷻ brought through it.

**For everyone:** The Arabs of Makkah sent their most beloved children into the desert — on purpose — because they knew that the open land, the hard life, and the pure language would make them stronger. What do we intentionally do today to prepare our children for the weight of life?

## ACTION POINT

This week, practise one thing that the desert years gave to Rasūlullāh ﷺ: **speak clearly and with care.**

Before family taʿlīm tonight, each person chooses one thing they are grateful to Allāh ﷻ for — and says it in one clear, complete sentence. No mumbling. No rushing. Speak as if the words matter — because they do.

If you have younger children, ask them: *"Who was Ḥalīmah?"* and let them explain it back to you in their own words. This is how knowledge becomes memory.

## NEXT LESSON PREVIEW

The years with Ḥalīmah were years of blessing. But they were also years of preparation — and the next event in the life of Rasūlullāh ﷺ would show that the preparation was more extraordinary than anyone in Banū Saʿd had imagined.

One afternoon, while the young Muḥammad ﷺ was playing near the tents with his foster-brother, two men appeared. They were dressed in white. They took him gently, laid him on his back, and opened his chest.

His foster-brother ran, screaming, for Ḥalīmah.

What had happened — and why — is the most astonishing event in the early life of Rasūlullāh ﷺ. It was not an accident. It was not a random event. It was Allāh ﷻ personally preparing the vessel that would carry the final revelation to all of humanity.

That is what we will explore in our next lesson: **The Opening of the Chest.**

## Event 1: The Custom of Desert Wet-Nursing Among the Arabs

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām (vol. 1); Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā — Ibn Saʿd (vol. 1)
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah — Ibn Kathīr; Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ — al-Dhahabī
**Authenticity Status:** Established — culturally and historically documented practice confirmed across multiple Sīrah sources
**Notes for Editors:** The practice of istiradāʿ (sending children to Bedouin wet-nurses) is confirmed as a widespread custom among noble Arab families. Rasūlullāh ﷺ himself later cited the clarity and purity of his Arabic as a result of his upbringing among Banū Saʿd. This is mentioned in narrations collected by Ibn Saʿd. The linguistic benefit is well-attested; some narrations attribute to the Prophet ﷺ himself the statement: "I am the most eloquent of the Arabs — I am from Quraysh, and I was suckled in Banū Saʿd ibn Bakr."

---

## Event 2: The Drought Year and the Difficult Journey of Ḥalīmah

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām (narrating from Ibn Isḥāq, from ʿAbdullāh ibn Jaʿfar, from Ḥalīmah's own account)
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report — transmitted through Ibn Isḥāq's chain; not in the canonical ḥadīth collections as a connected chain but accepted by major Sīrah scholars
**Notes for Editors:** The account of Ḥalīmah is primarily narrated in first person, transmitted by Ibn Isḥāq from Ḥalīmah (via a chain including ʿAbdullāh ibn Jaʿfar ibn Abī Ṭālib). Ibn Hishām preserves this in the Sīrah. Al-Ḥākim (al-Mustadrak) also narrates related material. The drought year and the slow donkey are consistently mentioned across these narrations. Some chains have been discussed by scholars of ḥadīth in terms of continuity — however, the overall narrative is accepted by the consensus of Sīrah scholars including Ibn Kathīr and al-Dhahabī.

---

## Event 3: Why the Women Rejected the Orphan Child

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām (Ḥalīmah's own narration via Ibn Isḥāq)
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report
**Notes for Editors:** The reason given in the sources is economic — the custom required the father to pay the wet-nurse, and an orphan had no father. This is explicitly stated in Ḥalīmah's account. Editors should note that this detail confirms the poverty/orphan status of Rasūlullāh ﷺ at birth and connects to the Quranic verse: "Did He not find you an orphan and give shelter?" (93:6). The connection between the historical report and the Quranic reference is meaningful but should not be conflated in the lesson text.

---

## Event 4: Ḥalīmah's Decision to Return and Take the Child

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report
**Notes for Editors:** In Ḥalīmah's narration, her motivation for returning is described as her not wanting to return home as the only woman without a child. Her husband's encouragement ("perhaps Allāh will bless us through him") is part of the same narration. This is a reported conversation within Ḥalīmah's first-person account — it is not an independently verified ḥadīth but is transmitted within the Sīrah chain as part of her direct testimony. The exact wording varies slightly between Ibn Hishām and Ibn Saʿd.

---

## Event 5: The Immediate Blessings — Milk, She-Camel, and Donkey

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām (Ḥalīmah's account)
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā (Ibn Saʿd)
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report
**Notes for Editors:** Three immediate blessings are described: (1) Ḥalīmah's own milk becoming full, (2) the she-camel giving milk when it had been dry, (3) the she-donkey becoming swift when it had been slow. All three appear in Ibn Isḥāq's narration of Ḥalīmah's account. The sequence is consistent across sources. The she-camel (nāqa) and she-donkey (ʿatān) are both mentioned — editors should note that some popular retellings conflate these into one animal, which is an error. They are two separate animals in the original narration.

---

## Event 6: The Barakah Witnessed by Banū Saʿd

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām (Ḥalīmah's account)
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report
**Notes for Editors:** The detail about the neighbouring families sending their flocks to graze where Ḥalīmah's flock grazed appears in Ibn Isḥāq's narration. This is part of Ḥalīmah's continuous first-person account and is accepted by Ibn Kathīr who includes it in Al-Bidāyah. The detail is not found in Bukhārī or Muslim but is consistent with the overall Sīrah narrative.

---

## Event 7: Āminah's Agreement to Extend the Nursing Period

**Primary Source:** Sīrah Ibn Hishām
**Supporting Sources:** Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah; Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā
**Authenticity Status:** Widely Accepted Sīrah Report
**Notes for Editors:** The extension of the nursing period beyond the initial two years is narrated in Ibn Isḥāq's account. Ḥalīmah's request to extend is presented as motivated by love for the child and concern for his health in Makkah. Āminah's agreement is noted but the exact conversation is not detailed in the sources. The total duration of Rasūlullāh ﷺ's time with Ḥalīmah varies between sources — some indicate two years (the standard nursing period), others indicate four years or slightly more. Ibn Saʿd gives the figure of around four to five years before the Shaqq al-Ṣadr event. Editors should note this variation and use cautious language ("approximately") in any factual summary.

---

## Duration and Age Timeline Note (for Editors)

The exact age of Rasūlullāh ﷺ at key events during the Ḥalīmah period is subject to scholarly variation:

- Standard nursing period: 2 years
- Extended stay: sources suggest an additional 1–2 years
- The Shaqq al-Ṣadr (not covered in this lesson) is placed by most sources at age 4–5

This lesson intentionally avoids specific age claims to prevent factual errors given this variation.
