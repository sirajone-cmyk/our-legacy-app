# SIRAJONE EDUCATIONAL FRAMEWORK
## 02 Writing Standards

**Status:** Permanent Reference  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Date Established:** June 2026

---

## OVERVIEW

These standards govern every word written in the OUR LEGACY series — Reader pages, Companion sections, Profiles, Map descriptions, Reference notes, and Discovery links. Any writer producing content for the project must read and apply every standard in this document before writing a single word.

---

## SECTION A — GENERAL WRITING STANDARDS

### A1 — Source Before Story
No event is narrated before its source has been identified and assigned a tier. The writing process is:
1. Identify the event.
2. Locate the primary classical source.
3. Assign the source a tier (see 05 Source Hierarchy).
4. Write the content at the confidence level appropriate to that tier.

The story never drives the source. The source always drives the story.

### A2 — One Point of View
The narrative is written from a position of respectful, informed historical narration. It does not use first-person (the narrator does not say "I") and does not use second-person singular ("you") except in direct reader address on Reflection pages and Companion sections. Third-person narrative is the standard register for Reader pages.

### A3 — Present Tense for Historical Narration
Reader segments use the present tense to create immediacy and engagement:
- "He is twenty-five years old and he has a reputation."
- "She does not hesitate. She sends Nafīsah to bring the answer back."

This is a deliberate stylistic choice. It makes the historical narrative feel alive without being sensational.

### A4 — Compassionate but Not Sentimental
The writing honours the emotional weight of events without becoming melodramatic. The rule: let the events carry the emotion, not the adjectives.

**Avoid:** "It was an incredibly heartbreaking and devastating moment for the young boy, who was utterly destroyed by grief."  
**Write:** "He was six years old. She was gone. He had no memory of his father. Now he had no mother either."

### A5 — Precision Over Vagueness
If a specific date, age, or figure is known with confidence, state it precisely. If it is disputed or uncertain, use hedged language (see Section C). Never use vague language where precision is available.

**Avoid:** "some years later"  
**Write:** "three years before the Hijrah" or "approximately the year 619 CE"

### A6 — Paragraph Length
No paragraph in Reader segments exceeds 5–6 sentences. Most paragraphs are 3–4 sentences. Dense, wall-of-text paragraphs are rejected at editorial review.

### A7 — Sentence Rhythm
Vary sentence length deliberately:
- Short sentences for impact.
- Medium sentences for explanation.
- Longer sentences only for complex historical context.

Begin key paragraphs with a strong, short sentence. End key paragraphs with a strong, short sentence.

### A8 — Active Voice Default
The writing uses active voice except where the passive voice genuinely clarifies who performed an action or who received it.

**Passive (avoid where possible):** "A proposal was sent to the Prophet ﷺ by Khadījah's intermediary."  
**Active (preferred):** "Khadījah sent Nafīsah to carry the proposal."

## SECTION B — ISLAMIC TERMINOLOGY STANDARDS

### B1 — Arabic Terms
Arabic terms are used where they are:
- Standard Islamic vocabulary that English-speaking Muslims use regularly (e.g., Ṣaḥābah, Sīrah, ḥadīth, nikāḥ).
- More precise than the English equivalent.
- Educationally valuable to introduce.

All Arabic terms are transliterated using the standard transliteration system (see Section D) and italicised on first use in each lesson, with an English explanation immediately following.

### B2 — Honorifics
The following honorifics are always used in the Reader text:
- **ﷺ** (ṣallallāhu ʿalayhi wa sallam) — always after the name of the Prophet Muḥammad.
- **ʿalayhi al-salām (عليه السلام)** — after the names of other prophets.
- **raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu / ʿanhā (رضي الله عنه / عنها)** — after the names of Companions and the Mothers of the Believers.

In digital app text, **ﷺ** is used as the glyph; it is not spelled out in full in the Reader segments.

### B3 — Qur'ānic Quotation
Qur'ānic verses are presented in three parts:
1. Arabic text (standard ʿUthmānic script).
2. English translation (following Saḥīḥ International or established scholarly translations, adjusted where necessary for accuracy and contemporary English).
3. Full reference: Surah name, Surah number, Verse number(s).

Example:
```
يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا اتَّقُوا اللَّهَ وَكُونُوا مَعَ الصَّادِقِينَ
"O you who have believed, fear Allah and be with those who are truthful."
Surah al-Tawbah, 9:119
```

### B4 — Ḥadīth Quotation
Authenticated ḥadīth are presented with:
1. A brief explanation of who is speaking or narrating.
2. The substance of the ḥadīth in English.
3. Source and ḥadīth number.

When a ḥadīth is quoted directly, the quote is clearly marked with quotation marks and attributed.

When paraphrasing the substance of a ḥadīth, paraphrase is clearly identified and the source is still cited.

**Critical rule:** Direct quotation from ḥadīth must be from an authenticated text. If the exact wording is unclear or disputed, use "the Prophet ﷺ said, in substance..." followed by the accurate paraphrase, with the citation.

### B5 — Names
Classical Arabic names are transliterated precisely in the Reader text:
- Muḥammad ﷺ (not Muhammad)
- Khadījah (not Khadija)
- ʿĀʾishah (not Aisha)
- Abū Ṭālib (not Abu Talib)
- ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib (not Abdul Muttalib)

**Exception:** The app's Chapters tab and chapter titles use simplified English spelling for accessibility (e.g., "Khadijah" in the chapter heading), with full transliteration inside the Reader text.

### B6 — "Allah" vs. "God"
The series consistently uses "Allah" — not "God." This is deliberate:
- "Allah" is a proper name, not a translation.
- The series is written for a Muslim audience who uses this name.
- "God" is used only in translated ḥadīth or Qur'ānic verses where the standard translation uses it.

## SECTION C — HEDGING LANGUAGE STANDARDS

Hedging language communicates to the reader the exact level of confidence the writer has in a claim. Using the wrong level of hedging — either overclaiming certainty or underclaiming what is well-established — is an editorial error.

### C1 — Tier 1 Claims (No Hedging Required)
Claims drawn directly from the Qur'ān, Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, or Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim are stated as established fact.

- "The Prophet ﷺ was born in the Year of the Elephant."
- "She believed in him when no one else did." *(Bukhārī 3816)*

### C2 — Tier 2 Claims (Soft Hedging)
Claims from Ibn Hishām, al-Ṭabarī, Ibn Saʿd, Dalāʾil al-Nubuwwah, etc. are presented with attribution or soft hedging:

- "Classical sources record that..."
- "Ibn Hishām narrates..."
- "The most widely accepted account holds that..."
- "It is reported by Ibn Isḥāq..."
- "According to the classical Sīrah..."

### C3 — Scholarly Disagreement
When classical scholars disagree on a specific detail (age, date, number), the following template is used:

1. State the dominant/majority position.
2. Acknowledge the existence of alternative positions.
3. Attribute the alternative positions to their sources.
4. Note why the dominant position is followed.

**Example from Lesson 10 (Khadījah's age at marriage):**
"The most widely transmitted classical position, found in Ibn Hishām, is that she was forty years old. Al-Ṭabarī and some other narrations suggest a younger age. The classical position of forty is followed by the majority of scholars. Whatever her age, the historical significance of the marriage is undisputed."

### C4 — Miraculous or Extraordinary Reports
Reports involving miracles, supernatural signs, or extraordinary events that appear in Tier 2 sources (not Qur'ān or the two Ṣaḥīḥs) are always:
1. Presented with their source named.
2. Hedged: "It is reported that..." / "Classical sources mention..."
3. Never presented as established historical fact equal to Tier 1 content.

**Example from Lesson 10 (Maysarah and the cloud):**
"Classical sources report that Maysarah observed something remarkable during the journey: a cloud that followed Muhammad ﷺ to shade him in the desert heat. This narration is preserved in the classical Sīrah tradition."

## SECTION D — TRANSLITERATION STANDARDS

The project uses a modified scholarly transliteration system accessible to English readers who do not read Arabic, while preserving precision for those who do.

### Core transliteration rules:
- **ā** = long alif (ā in Muḥammad, Khadījah)
- **ī** = long yā (ī in Sīrah, Khadījah)
- **ū** = long wāw (ū in Nubuwwah)
- **ʿ** = ʿayn (ʿUmar, ʿAlī, ʿĀʾishah)
- **ʾ** = hamza (Qurʾān, Iqraʾ)
- **ṣ** = ṣād (Ṣaḥīḥ, Ṣaḥābah)
- **ḥ** = ḥāʾ (Muḥammad, ḥadīth)
- **ḍ** = ḍād (Ramaḍān)
- **ṭ** = ṭāʾ (Ṭāhir, Abū Ṭālib)
- **ẓ** = ẓāʾ
- **th** = thāʾ (Thabāt)
- **kh** = khāʾ (Khadījah)
- **dh** = dhāl (Adhān)
- **gh** = ghayn (Ghazwah)
- **sh** = shīn (Shām)

Transliteration is used consistently in all Reader segments. For app UI elements (chapter headings, navigation), simplified English spelling may be used for readability.

## SECTION E — WHAT IS NEVER WRITTEN

### E1 — Never fabricate dialogue
If the Prophet ﷺ or any historical figure did not say something in an authenticated source, they do not say it in the Reader. Events may be narrated, but words are never invented.

**Wrong:** "The Prophet ﷺ turned to his uncle and said, 'I will never give up this mission.'"  
**Right:** "The Prophet ﷺ refused. The message was not his to withdraw. *(Reference: historical narration of meeting with Abū Ṭālib)*"

### E2 — Never speculate about internal states
The Reader does not speculate about what historical figures "must have felt" or "surely thought" unless this is directly attested.

**Wrong:** "Khadījah must have been nervous when she sent Nafīsah with the proposal."  
**Right:** "She sent Nafīsah to bring the answer back."

### E3 — Never include rejected or weak narrations as fact
A ḍaʿīf narration that contradicts stronger evidence is never used, even if it makes for a more compelling story.

### E4 — Never anachronise
Do not apply modern concepts, emotions, or frameworks to 7th-century Arabia. The subjects lived in a specific historical context. That context is respected.

### E5 — Never use dramatic rhetorical questions in Reader text
"Who could have imagined what would happen next?" — this style is never used in Reader segments. The narrative is confident and direct.

### E6 — Never introduce Lesson 11+ content in earlier lessons
Structural continuity (Master Principle 5) is strictly enforced. If a lesson approaches the First Revelation, it ends with anticipation. The revelation scene waits for its own lesson.

## SECTION F — EDITORIAL REVIEW STANDARDS

Before any new lesson is submitted for deployment, it must pass the following writing review:

1. **Source check** — every factual claim has been verified against its cited source.
2. **Hedge level check** — every claim is hedged at the correct level for its tier.
3. **Quotation check** — every direct quotation is from an authenticated text, not paraphrased and presented as a quote.
4. **Continuity check** — the lesson does not narrate events belonging to future lessons.
5. **Virtue alignment check** — the virtue in each segment is genuinely illustrated by the narrative in that segment.
6. **Tone check** — the writing is precise, dignified, and readable.
7. **Length check** — each segment is 10 minutes at average reading speed (approximately 1,200–1,500 words for non-specialist readers).
8. **Honorific check** — all honorifics are correctly placed.
9. **Transliteration check** — all Arabic names are correctly transliterated.
10. **nextLessonPreview check** — the preview anticipates the next lesson without narrating it.
