## CORE QUESTION THIS LESSON ANSWERS

What happened on the journey with Abū Ṭālib to Shām (Syria)? What do the classical sources establish about the encounter with Baḥīrā the monk, and what is the educational significance of this account?

---

## KEY SOURCES CONSULTED

- Sīrat Ibn Hishām — the full account of the journey with Abū Ṭālib and the Baḥīrā encounter (Tier 2)
- Al-Ṭabarī — the Baḥīrā account (Tier 2)
- Ibn Saʿd — the trade journey and guardianship period (Tier 2)
- Sūnan al-Tirmidhī — authenticated ḥadīth related to Abū Ṭālib's protection of the Prophet ﷺ (Tier 2 — Tirmidhī is classified below the two Ṣaḥīḥs but above T3 for this project)
- Qur'ān — Sūrat al-Aʿrāf 7:157 ("those who follow the unlettered Prophet, whom they find written with them in the Torah and the Injīl") — the scriptural dimension of Baḥīrā's recognition (Tier 1)

---

## RESEARCH QUESTIONS RAISED DURING WRITING

**1. The Baḥīrā account — what exactly does Ibn Hishām claim?**
Resolved: Ibn Hishām narrates that Baḥīrā (a Christian monk of the city of Buṣrā) saw the young Muḥammad ﷺ in the trade caravan and recognised in him the signs of prophethood he had read of in his scriptures. He warned Abū Ṭālib to take the boy back to Mecca to protect him from those who might harm him. This is narrated as Ibn Hishām's account, not as an authenticated ḥadīth from the Ṣaḥīḥayn.

**2. Is the Baḥīrā account in any Ṣaḥīḥ sources?**
Resolved: The Baḥīrā encounter is found in Sīrat Ibn Hishām and al-Ṭabarī (both T2). It is not in Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī or Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim at this level of detail. Tirmidhī has related narrations. The lesson narrates this account clearly attributed to Ibn Hishām, with the appropriate hedging: "Ibn Hishām narrates that..." The educational significance is presented as an illustration of the divine protection around the Prophet ﷺ before prophethood.

**3. Should the Baḥīrā account be included at all given its Tier 2 status?**
Resolved: Yes. The account is from the primary Sīrah source (Ibn Hishām), is widely transmitted in classical literature, and the Qur'ānic principle of prophetic recognition in scriptures is Tier 1 (7:157). The lesson presents the event as a Sīrah narration (T2), not as a mutawātir ḥadīth (T1). The reader is equipped to understand the distinction.

**4. What was the purpose of the journey?**
Resolved: Abū Ṭālib was a trader. The journey to Shām (Syria) was a trade journey — the same route the Quraysh regularly used (the summer and winter journeys, referenced in Sūrat Quraysh, 106:1–2). The Prophet ﷺ, now approximately twelve years old, accompanied his guardian for the first time. This is the educational context: the future husband of Khadījah and the later trade journeys all begin with this earliest training in the Meccan commercial world.

**5. What was Shām in the 6th century?**
Resolved: Shām referred to the region known as Greater Syria — encompassing modern Syria, Lebanon, and parts of Jordan and Palestine. Buṣrā (the city where Baḥīrā resided) was a major Christian trading centre at the northern end of the Ḥijāz trade route. A Maps tab entry for Shām/Buṣrā was flagged during writing.

## SOURCES ACCEPTED

- The journey to Shām with Abū Ṭālib (~age 12): Ibn Hishām (T2), Ibn Saʿd (T2). Consistent.
- The Baḥīrā encounter: Ibn Hishām (T2), al-Ṭabarī (T2). Narrated with attribution.
- Qur'ān 106:1–2 (the Quraysh trade journeys): T1. Used for context on the trading world.
- Qur'ān 7:157 (the Prophet ﷺ found in the earlier scriptures): T1. Used to establish the Qur'ānic basis for Baḥīrā's recognition.

## EDITORIAL DECISIONS MADE DURING THIS LESSON

- Virtue: ṣidq (truthfulness) and amānah (trustworthiness) — the lesson focuses on the young Muḥammad's ﷺ already emerging character, which led Baḥīrā to recognise him as the expected prophet.
- The lesson deliberately does not over-claim about Baḥīrā's account. It states what the Sīrah sources record; it does not turn a Tier 2 account into a Tier 1 theological certainty.
- The return from Shām is not narrated in detail — the lesson focuses on the journey and the Baḥīrā encounter, ending with Abū Ṭālib taking the child home.
