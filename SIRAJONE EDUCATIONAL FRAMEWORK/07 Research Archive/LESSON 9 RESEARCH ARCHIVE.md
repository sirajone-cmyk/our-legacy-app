## CORE QUESTION THIS LESSON ANSWERS

What was Ḥilf al-Fuḍūl? Who formed it, why was it formed, what did the Prophet ﷺ later say about it, and what does it teach us about justice, honour, and civic responsibility in Islamic ethics?

---

## KEY SOURCES CONSULTED

- Sīrat Ibn Hishām — the formation of the pact, who participated, and the occasion that triggered it (Tier 2)
- Al-Ṭabarī — supplementary account of the pact's context (Tier 2)
- Ibn Saʿd — details of the participants and the pact's terms (Tier 2)
- Ṣaḥīḥ narrations attributed to the Prophet ﷺ himself about the pact — his statement after prophethood, narrated via multiple channels including Ibn Isḥāq as transmitted by Ibn Hishām

## RESEARCH QUESTIONS RAISED DURING WRITING

**1. What were the exact terms of Ḥilf al-Fuḍūl?**
Resolved: Ibn Hishām narrates that the signatories agreed to stand with any oppressed person in Mecca, regardless of tribal affiliation, and to restore rights that had been wrongfully taken. The pact was specifically triggered by the case of a merchant from Yemen who had his goods seized by a Meccan man and could not get justice through the normal tribal system. This triggering event is narrated in the lesson.

**2. Why was it called Ḥilf al-Fuḍūl?**
Resolved: Ibn Hishām gives two explanations: (a) it was a revival of an older pact among the Fuḍūl (men named Faḍl), or (b) it was called the Pact of Virtues/Excellences (al-fuḍūl meaning "excellences" in one reading). Both explanations are mentioned in the lesson. The lesson follows the more common explanation — the pact of those who are virtuous.

**3. The Prophet's ﷺ statement about the pact AFTER Islam.**
Resolved: The Prophet ﷺ is reported to have said (attributed via Ibn Hishām): "I witnessed in the house of ʿAbdullāh ibn Judʿān a pact which, if I were invited to such a thing in Islam, I would accept." This is one of the most significant statements about pre-Islamic virtue in the Sīrah tradition. It is narrated with attribution (not in the Ṣaḥīḥayn as a ḥadīth with full isnād chain, but transmitted consistently in the Sīrah literature).

**4. How old was the Prophet ﷺ at the time of the pact?**
Resolved: Classical sources generally place this approximately in his early-to-mid teens, around the age of 14–17. The exact year is not firmly established. The lesson uses "in his teenage years" rather than committing to a specific age.

**5. Should the lesson address the broader question of "can a Muslim honour a pre-Islamic institution"?**
Resolved: Yes — but not as a fiqh question. The lesson makes the educational point that the Prophet ﷺ himself affirmed that justice and virtue are not exclusive to Islam. The Ḥilf al-Fuḍūl is an example of a moral order that Islam came to perfect, not to erase. This is grounded in the Prophet's ﷺ own statement about the pact.

## EDITORIAL DECISIONS MADE DURING THIS LESSON

- Virtue: ʿadl (justice) and nusrah (standing with the oppressed) — this lesson teaches that the young Muḥammad ﷺ was, even before prophethood, a person who took active steps against injustice, not just a passive witness to it.
- The lesson explicitly notes that the pact predates Islam but that the Prophet ﷺ honoured it — this is educationally important for readers who may believe Islam requires rejecting all pre-Islamic structures.
- The lesson keeps the historical narrative tight: the occasion, the pact, the Prophet's ﷺ participation, the later statement. It does not expand into tangential Meccan political history.
